5. Nuclear Physics

5.1: The Nuclear Model of the Atom

  • (5.1.1) The Atom
    • The Structure of the Atom
      • Atoms are formed by a positively charged nucleus and negatively charged electrons in orbit around the nucleus
        • The nucleus is made up of protons & neutrons
    • The Formation of Ions
      • An Ion is an electrically charged atom or group of atoms formed by the loss or gain of electrons (to obtain a duplet or octet structure)
        • Losing electrons = Positive ion (cation)
        • Gaining electrons = Negative ion (anion)
    • Rutherford’s Experiment
      • In 1909, a group of scientists (including Ernest Rutherford) fired a beam of alpha particles (He²⁺, or 2 protons & 2 neutrons) towards a thin gold foil
        • Gold is the most malleable metal and formed the thinnest layer of metal
        • This was done to investigate the Plum Pudding model (a cloud of positive matter with electrons scattered inside, like plums in a pudding)
      DiscoveryInference
      Most alpha particles passed straight through the foila very small nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space
      The alpha particles were backscattered (or reflected at a large angle)a nucleus containing most of the mass of the atom (heavier than an alpha particle at least)
      Some (positively charged) alpha particles were deflecteda nucleus that is positively charged
  • (5.1.2) Protons, Neutrons & Electrons
    • Relative Charge
      Proton +1
      Neutron 0
      Electron -1
    • Nuclide Notation
      • Proton/atomic Number (Z) = the number of protons in an atom
      • Nucleon/mass Number (A) = the total number of particles (p+n) in the nucleus
    • Isotopes
      • Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have an equal number of protons but a different number of neutrons
        • An element may have more than one isotope
  • (5.1.3) Nuclear Fission

        • Corrected count rate = readings - background radiation (by removing source)
  • (5.2.2) Types of Nuclear Emission
    • Alpha, Beta(-) & Gamma Particles
      • Ionisation is the process of an atom gaining or losing electrons to become charged
        • Greater charge = greater ionisation (α>β>γ)
        • Greater kinetic energy (hence mass) = greater ionisation (α>β>γ)

    • Deflection in Electric & Magnetic Fields
      • Electric Fields
        • Alpha particles are heavier than beta particles and deflect less
          • Alpha(+2) particles are deflected towards the negative plate
          • Beta(-1) particles are deflected towards the positive plate
        • Gamma radiation is not deflected and travels straight
      • Magnetic Fields
        • Alpha and beta particles are also deflected by magnetic fields (whilst moving)
          • Alpha Particle = left-hand Fleming’s
          • Beta Particle = right-hand Fleming’s (normal)
  • (5.2.3) Radioactive Decay
    • Causes of Radioactive Decay
      • Some atomic nuclei are unstable due to having too many protons or neutrons
        • Unstable nuclei can emit radiation (decay) to become more stable
        • Radioactive decay is spontaneous and random in direction
      • Isotopes of an element may be radioactive due to an excess of neutrons in the nucleus and/or the nucleus being too heavy
    • Types of Radioactive Decay
      • α-decay
        • Reduces the number of excess neutrons
        • Nucleus changes to that of a different element
        • Releases an alpha particle (2 protons, 2 neutrons)
      • β-decay
        • Reduces the number of excess neutrons
        • Nucleus changes to that of a different element
        • Neutron —> Proton + Electron
          • Releases a beta particle (1 electron)
      • γ-radiation
        • Reduces excess energy via gamma ray
        • Nucleus DOES NOT change to that of a different element
  • (5.2.4) Half-Lives
    • The half-life of a particular isotope is the time taken for half the nuclei of that isotope in any sample to decay
      • Proportion of isotope remaining for half-life (x) is 2−x2^{-x}2−x﻿

  • (5.2.6) Safety Precautions
    • The Effects of Ionising Nuclear Radiation
      • Ionising radiation can damage living things at high doses
        • If the atoms in a DNA strand are ionised, the DNA strand can be damaged:
          1. Cell death: the cell may simply die
          1. Mutations: the DNA may make errors when trying to reform itself
          1. Cancer: if a mutated cell is able to replicate, it becomes cancer
    • Handling Radioactive Materials
      Storagein a lead-lined container
      Removaluse forceps/tongs, never bare hands
      Distance from Selfas far as possible
      Duration of Useas short as possible
      Personal Protectionuse gloves, wear protective clothing, and stay behind a lead shield
