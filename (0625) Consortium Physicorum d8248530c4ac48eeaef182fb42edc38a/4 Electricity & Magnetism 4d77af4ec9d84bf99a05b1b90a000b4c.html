4. Electricity & Magnetism

4.1: Simple Phenomena of Magnetism

  • (4.1.1) Magnetism
    • Magnets have ends called poles, 1 North, 1 South (N pole, S pole)
      • Like poles repel (repulsion)
      • Unlike poles attract (attraction)
  • (4.1.2) Magnets
    • Non-Magnetic VS Magnetic Materials
      • Non-magnetic materials are not attracted to magnets
      • Magnetic materials are attracted to either pole when unmagnetised
        • Become magnets when magnetised (induced magnetism)
        • Include Iron, Cobalt & Nickel
    • Permanent & Induced Magnetsd
      MaterialPhenomenonDurability
      Permanent permanent magnetic material (steel)produces its own magnetic fielddoes not lose magnetism
      Induced magnetic material (soft iron)becomes magnetised when placed within a magnetic field when removed, loses most/all of its magnetism quickly

      *Magnetisation: one end becomes an N pole, one end becomes an S pole

    • Applications of Permanent & Electromagnets
      Permanent MagnetsCan be tested by placed two next to each other (repel)

      - School lab experiments
      - Compasses
      - Toy trains
      - Fridge magnets
      ElectromagnetsCan be switched on and off
      - MRI scanners
      - Speakers & earphones
      - Recycling (separating steel)
      - Mag-Lev Trains
  • (4.1.3) Magnetic Fields
    • Describing Magnetic Fields
      • Magnetic field = a region in which a magnetic pole experiences a force
        • Magnetic forces are due to interactions between magnetic fields
      • The direction of a magnetic field at a point is the direction of the force on the N pole of a magnet at that point (imagine a tiny N dot)
      • The relative strength of a magnetic field is represented by the spacing of the magnetic field lines
        • The further away (usually), the further apart the spacing
    • Plotting Magnetic Fields
      1. Iron filings (sprinkle on paper and then tap paper)
      1. Plotting compass (dots and end-to-end)

4.2: Electrical Quantities

  • (4.2.1) Charge
    • Electric Charge
      • Measured in coulombs (6.24×10186.24 \times 10^{18}6.24×1018﻿ electrons)
        • Charges can be positive or negative
          • Like charges repel
          • Opposite charges attract
    • Electric Fields
      • The direction of an electric field at a point is the direction of the force on a positive charge at that point
        • (a) around a point charge
        • (b) around a charged conducting sphere
        • (c) between two oppositely charged parallel conducting plates (end effects will not be examined)
    • Investigating Electrostatic Charge & Friction
      • The charging of solids by friction involves only a transfer of negative charge (electrons)
        • Only insulating materials can be charged by friction
        • Polythene gains electrons from cloth
        • Perspex loses electrons to cloth
      • Test electrostatic charge by hanging rods with string and edging them
    • Investigating Conductors & Insulators
      • Electronic Structures
        • Conductors are made up of a lattice of positive ions, in a sea of delocalised electrons that can carry charge
          • Metals, e.g. Silver, Copper, Gold, Graphite (Carbon, non-metal)
        • Insulators do not have free charges
          • (most) Non-Metals, Air, Glass, Rubber, etc.
      • Electroscopes
  • (4.2.2) Current
    • Electric Current
      • Electric current is the charge passing a point per unit time
        • I=QtI = {\frac{Q}{t}}I=tQ​﻿ —> [I] Current (A) = [Q] Charge (C) / [t] Time (s)
    • Types of Current
      • Current in Conductive Metals
        • In metals, the current is caused by a flow of free (delocalised) electrons
      • Conventional Current
      • Direct Current (d.c.) VS Alternating Current (a.c.)
        • d.c. = one way, produced by dry cells & batteries
        • a.c. = alternates (usually 50 Hz), produced by mains & generators
          • Easier to generate, can carry charge over longer distances
    • Ammeters (A)
      • All charge should flow through it (usually near the terminals)
      • Always connected in series
      • Black = Negative / Red = Positive
        • Analogue ammeters = 0.1-1.0A / 1.0-5.0 A
          • Subject to zero error (no tare) & parallax error
        • Digital ammeters = small currents (mA or µA)
          • Subject to zero error & flickering
  • (4.2.3) E.M.F. & P.D.
    • Electromotive Force
      • Electromotive Force = the electrical work done by a source in moving a unit charge around a complete circuit; in volts
        • E=WQE = {\frac{W}{Q}}E=QW​﻿ —> [E] e.m.f (V) = [W] Energy supplied (J) / [Q] Charge (C)
        • From the power supply
    • Potential Difference
      • Potential Difference = the electrical work done by a unit charge passing through a component; in volts
        • V=WQV = {\frac{W}{Q}}V=QW​﻿ —> [V] P.D. (V) = [W] Energy Transferred (J) / [Q] Charge (C)
        • Between two points
    • Voltmeters (V)
      • Always connected in parallel with the component
      • Black = Negative / Red = Positive
        • Analogue voltmeters = 0.1-1.0 V / 1.0-5.0 V
          • Subject to zero error (no tare) & parallax error
        • Digital voltmeters = small p.d.s (mV or µV)
          • Subject to zero error & flickering
  • (4.2.4) Resistance
    • Ohm’s Law (Ω)
      • Resistance is the opposition to current, and can be used to control current
        • R=VIR = {\frac{V}{I}}R=IV​﻿ —> [R] Resistance (Ω) = [V] P.D. (V) / [I] Current (A)
        • Ohmic = proportional
        • Non-ohmic = non-proportional (varying resistance)
    • Current-Voltage Graphs
      • x-axis: p.d. / y-axis: current
      • (a) a resistor of constant resistance
      • (b) a filament lamp (non-ohmic)
        • Filament lamps increase in temperature (hence resistance) when more current is applied
      • (c) a diode (non-ohmic)
    • Investigating Resistance
    • Resistance in Metals
      • Resistance is directly proportional to length
      • Resistance is inversely proportional to cross-sectional area
  • (4.2.5) Energy & Power
    • Electrical Transfer
      • Electric circuits transfer energy from a source of electrical energy (such as an
        electrical cell or mains supply
        ) to the circuit components and then into the surroundings
        • Electrical Power
          • P=IVP = IVP=IV﻿ —> [P] Power (W) = [I] Current (A) * [V] P.D. (V)
        • Electrical Energy
          • E=IVtE = IVtE=IVt﻿ —> [E] Transfer (J) = [I] Current (A) * [V] P.D. (V) * [t] Time (s)
    • The Kilowatt Hour
      • The Kilowatt Hour = a unit of energy equivalent to one kilowatt of power expended for one hour
        • Amount of energy transferred to the device / second
        • To calculate energy usage in home & business appliances
        • Joule = kWh * (3.6 * 10^6)

  • (4.3.1) Circuit Diagrams & Components
  • (4.3.2) Series & Parallel Circuits
    • Current in Series Circuits
      • Current at every point in a series circuit = the same
    • Current in Parallel Circuits
      • Sum of the currents entering a junction = Sum of the currents leaving
        • Due to the conservation of charge
        • Current from source > Current in each branch
    • P.D. in Series Circuits
      • Total P.D. = the sum of the individual P.D.s across each component
        • Also equal to the total E.M.F.
    • P.D. in Parallel Circuits
      • Total P.D. = P.D. across one branch
    • Resistance in Series Circuits
      • Combined resistance = Sum of individual resistances
    • Resistance in Parallel Circuits
      • The combined resistance of two resistors in parallel is less than that of either resistor by itself
    • Advantages of Parallel Lamp Circuits
      • Components can be individually controlled, using their own switches
      • If one component stops working the others will continue to function
  • (4.3.3) Potential Dividers
    • Ohm’s Law → P.D. increases as resistance increases, for a constant current

4.4: Electrical Safety

  • (4.4.1) Mains Electricity
    • Common Hazards
      Damaged Insulationexposed wires can lethally shock a person
      Overheating Cablespassing too much current through a small (or tightly coiled) wire can cause a fire or melt the insulations
      Damp Conditionsmoisture conducts electricity, which can cause a short circuit (path of least resistance), and overheat cables (see above) or pose an electrocution risk
      Excess Current;
      from
      overloading plugs, extension leads, single or multiple sockets      		If plugs or sockets become overloaded due to plugging in too many components the heat created can cause fires
    • UK Mains Electricity
      • Mains electricity is the electricity generated by power stations and transported around the country through the National Grid
        • Alternating Current (a.c.) supply
        • Frequency of 50 Hz
        • Voltage of 230 V
    • Mains Circuit Components
      • Alternating current does not have “negative” and “positive”
        • Live = “negative” (brown is the colour of the shit in your pants if you ever touch it)
        • Neutral = “positive” (completes the circuit, 0 p.d.)
        • Earth = for safety (if casing is metallic, path of least resistance to the earth)
  • (4.4.2) Safety Features of Electrical Appliances
    • Switches
      • A switch must be connected to the live wire for the circuit to be switched off safely
    • Fuses
      • Safety device designed to cut off the flow of electricity to an appliance if the current becomes too large (due to a fault or a surge)
        • Usually consist of a glass cylinder which contains a thin metal wire
        • The wire heats up, melts, and breaks if the current becomes too large
    • Trip Switches (Circuit Breakers)
      • Found in the fuse box
      • Does the same thing as a fuse, but can be flicked back on (reusable)
    • Double Insulation
      • Wires are coated in plastic, then wrapped in rubber/plastic again
    • Earthing
      • For electrical appliances with metal cases
        • Can become “live” if the live wire becomes exposed
        • The casing would become electrified and could electrocute someone
      • The earth wire provides a path of least resistance to the earth
        • The current runs into the ground
        • Once the current runs for a short while, the fuse will have had enough electricity pass through it to break
    • Fuse/Trip Switch Ratings
      • Always have a current rating higher than the current needed by the appliance
        • 3A → 5A → 13A

4.5: Electromagnetic Effects

(Know that every “current” referred to in this chapter is CONVENTIONAL CURRENT)

  • (4.5.3) Magnetic Effect of a Current
    • Current be making magnetic fields blawg
    • In Straight Wires (Right-Hand Palm Rule)
    • In Solenoids (N&S Rule)
    • Factors Affecting the Induced Magnetic Field
      • Magnetic field reverses when current reverses
      • Magnetic field strength decreases with distance from wire
      • Magnetic field strength increases with current (0 if no current)
      • Magnetic field strength increases with the number of coils
    • Applications of the Magnetic Effect
      • Induced Magnetism
        • A solenoid can be induced into a magnet
          • Electromagnet = soft iron core
          • Permanent Magnet = steel core
      • Relays (and bells)
        • Relays are switches that open and close via the action of an electromagnet
      • Loudspeakers
        • Loudspeakers convert electrical signals into sound via converting alternating current into oscillations that produce sound waves
    • Investigating the Magnetic Effect
      • Always add a variable resistor and ammeter to the circuit
      • In Straight Wires
        • Put the wire perpendicularly through a piece of paper

  • (4.5.4) Force on Current-Carrying Conductors (Motor Effect)
    • The Motor Effect
      • The magnetic effect produced by a current causes a force in the current-carrying conductor when inside a magnetic field
      • Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule
        • To find the relative direction of produced force based on the directions of the current and magnetic field
    • Factors Affecting the Produced Force
      • Magnetic field reverses when current reverses
      • Magnetic field reverses when the magnetic field reverses
      • Produced force magnitude increases with the strength of the magnetic field
      • Produced force magnitude increases with the number of turns on the coils of wire
      • Produced force magnitude increases with the size of the coils
      • Produced force magnitude increases with the current
    • Demonstrating the Motor Effect
    • Force on Charged Particles
  • (4.5.1) Electromagnetic Induction (Generator Effect)
    • An EMF will be induced in a conductor if there is relative movement between the conductor and a magnetic field
    • Lenz’s Law
      • Lenz’s Law: the direction of an induced E.M.F. always opposes the change
        • The induced EMF direction correlates with the induced magnetic effect
        • In Solenoids
        • In Wires (Fleming’s Right-Hand Rule)
          • When a wire is inside a magnetic field, any force applied on it is opposed (according to Lenz’s Law), and this “against force” is generated by an induced current, but because this induced current produces an opposing force, you have to use another rule
            • To find the relative direction of induced current based on the directions of the (applied) force and magnetic field
              • Induced current also implies electrons
    • Factors Affecting the Induced EMF
      • Magnetic field reverses when current reverses
      • Magnetic field reverses when the magnetic field reverses
      • Induced EMF magnitude increases with speed of relative movement
      • Induced EMF magnitude increases with the number of turns on the coils of wire
      • Induced EMF magnitude increases with the size of the coils
      • Induced EMF magnitude increases with the strength of the magnetic field
    • Demonstrating the Generator Effect
      • Straight Wire Experiment
      • Solenoid Experiment

    • Simple Transformers
      • A transformer is an electrical device that can be used to increase or decrease the P.D. of an alternating current (voltage transformations/transmissions)

        • The alternating magnetic field in the first coil (produced by the motor effect) passes through the soft iron and induces P.D. in the secondary coil

      • The output potential difference (voltage) of a transformer depends on:
        • The number of turns on the primary and secondary coils

    • Power-Conservative Calculations
      • An ideal transformer would be 100% efficient
        • Although transformers can increase the voltage of a power source, due to the law of conservation of energy, they cannot increase the power output
        • Therefore current would have to decrease (or increase)
        • Input power = Output power (rmb power = IV)
    • Power Loss Calculations
      • Current creates heat in wires due to resistance, which reduces transformer efficiency
        • P=I2×RP = I^2 \times RP=I2×R﻿ —> [P] Power Loss (W) = [I] Current (A) * [R] Resistance (Ω)

          (1 Watt = 1 Joule/second; power is energy per second)

        • E=P×tE = P \times tE=P×t﻿ —> [E] Total Energy Loss (J) = [P] Power Loss (W) * [t] Time (s)
    • Advantages of High-Voltage Transmission
      • When electricity is transmitted over large distances, the current in the wires heats them, resulting in energy loss
      • To transmit the same amount of power as the input power the potential difference at which the electricity is transmitted should be increased
        • This will result in a smaller current being transmitted through the power lines
        • This is because P = IV, so if V increases, I decreases for the same power
        • A smaller current will reduce the energy loss in the power lines
    • Applications of Transformers
      • To increase the P.D. of electricity before it is transmitted across the national grid
      • To lower the high voltage electricity in mains electricity
