  • (1.2.4) Free Fall
    • Newtonian Gravitational Acceleration
      • The acceleration of free fall (g) for all objects near to the surface of the Earth is approximately constant and is approximately 9.8 m/s¬≤
        • Within Earth‚Äôs uniform gravitational field
        • Without air resistance
        • For every second an object falls, its velocity will increase by 9.8 m/s
    • The Action of Air Resistance
      • Objects falling through fluids experience two forces:
        1. Weight (from gravitation)
        1. Friction (e.g. air resistance)
      • Weight will naturally balance out with friction, resulting in zero acceleration, but NOT zero speed ‚Äî> TERMINAL VELOCITY
        • Acceleration increases speed
        • Speed increases friction
          • Surface area also increases friction, hence the force of air resistance is greater than the force of weight when a parachute opens, and it gradually balances out the opposite way
        • Friction decreases acceleration
        • Decreased acceleration reduces the increase of speed

1.5: Forces

  • (1.5.1) Effects of Forces
    • The Basic Force
      • Force is a push or pull that acts on an object, due to the interaction with another object
        • Measured in newtons (N)
        • May produce, within an object:
          1. A Change in Speed
          1. A Change in Direction
          1. A Change in Size/Shape
          1. Rotation
    • Resultant Forces
      • Resultant force is the singular force that describes all the forces operating on a body
        • Determines, within an object:
          1. The Direction in which the object will move as a result of all the forces
          1. The Magnitude of the final force experienced by the object
        Balanced ForcesAll forces have combined in such a way that they cancel each other out ‚Äî> NO resultant force acts on the body
        Unbalanced ForcesAll forces have combined in such a way that they do not cancel out completely ‚Äî> there is a resultant force on the object
  • (1.5.2) Newton‚Äôs Laws of Motion
    • Newton‚Äôs 1st Law of Motion (Inertia)
      • ‚ÄúA body remains at rest, or in motion at a constant velocity, unless acted upon by a (resultant) force‚Äù
        • An object will remain stationary if it was stationary before

          An object will continue to move at the same velocity if it was moving before

    • Newton‚Äôs 2nd Law of Motion (Force)
      • ‚ÄúWhen a body is acted upon by a net force, the body's acceleration multiplied by its mass is equal to the net force‚Äù ‚Äî> F=maF=maF=maÔªø
        • F ¬†= resultant force on the object (N)
        • m = mass of the object (kg)
        • a ¬†= acceleration of the object (m/s2)
      • [!] Resultant force & acceleration act in the same direction
  • (1.5.3) Friction
    • Solid Friction is the force between two surfaces that may impede motion and produce heating (energy is transferred in the form of heat)
      • Caused by imperfections in the surfaces (push against each other on a molecular level)
    • Drag (Friction) acts on an object moving through a fluid (liquid/gas)
      • Caused by particles colliding with the object (e.g. air resistance)
      • Also produces heat

1.7: Energy, Work & Power

  • (1.7.1) Energy
    • Energy as a Property
      • Energy is a property that must be transferred to an object to perform work on or heat up that object
        • Measured in units of joules (J)
      • Energy is often described as part of an energy system (an object or group of objects)
        • To narrow the parameters to focus only on what is relevant
        • In equilibrium, energy is only transferred when change is applied to the system
    • Energy Stores
      KineticWhen objects are moving
      Grav. PotentialWhen objects are lifted above ground
      ElasticWhen objects are stretched
      ElectrostaticWhen objects with charges move in opposition to the repel/attract thing
      MagneticWhen magnetic poles move in opposition to the repel/attract thing
      ChemicalReleased in chemical reactions
      NuclearReleased in nuclear reactions (released by atomic nuclei)
      ThermalIn all objects; scales with temperature
    • Energy Transfer Pathways
      Mechanical WorkingWhen a force acts on an object
      Electrical WorkingA charge (current) moving through a potential difference (e.g. circuit)
      HeatingHot to cold
      WavesTransferred by electromagnetic, light, sound, etc. waves
    • Energy Resources
      • The Sun is the main source of energy for all energy resources
        • Except geothermal, nuclear & tidal
        • Research is being carried out to investigate how energy released by nuclear fusion can be used to produce electrical energy on a large scale
    • Conservation of Energy
      • Energy cannot be created nor destroyed, only transferred from one store to another
        • i.e. in a closed system, the total amount of energy is constant
        • Energy Flow Diagrams = stores (boxes) + transfers (arrows or arrows with boxes)
          • Energy change = when the source of store changes
